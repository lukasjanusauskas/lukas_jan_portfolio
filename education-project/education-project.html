Analysis Notebook

The following notebook illustrates advanced analytical techniques paired with sociopolitical/macroecnomic context create meaningful insights.

Analysis conclusions:

  • Tertiary education levels have a stronger relationship with activity rates than secondary or primary education levels.
  • Higher education expenditure seems to correlate mostly with tertiary education levels more than secondary education levels (percentage of residents that have reached seccondary education and not higher).
  • While education expenditure can influence better outcomes, in some cases simply pouring money is not the best course of action, if the assets are not used well.

Context, case studies:

  • Malta had an exceptionally large education expenditure compared to their GDP. This is explained by these main factors: (1) Malta is a small island that largely depends on its human resources, skilled labour (2) growing immigrant population and subsequent educational challenges.
  • Turkey has very small activity levels for many reasons, two of which are: (1) small female participation in the labour market (2) small expenditure on social programmes, especially on education.
  • Factors such as low teacher pay led to a large pupil to teacher ratio in Slovenia. This most likely did negatively impact overall performance and education quality. However the problem lies not in small expenditure, since the expenditure is even slightly bigger than European median. The problem might be in poor managament of said expenditure.
